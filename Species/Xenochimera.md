# Xenochimera

<img src="../assets/raptor_idle_south.png" width="96" align="right">

Extraterrestrial colony organisms composed of microscopic hive organisms capable of consuming and mimicking the cellular structure of other life forms, xenochimera attain sapience by assimilating sufficiently complex nervous systems and are prone to feral behavioral regression under stress.

## Biology

Xenochimera bodies consist of a hive of microscopic organisms. These cells communicate with adjacent cells to form a primitive distributed neural network, but individual cells are not capable of intelligence beyond basic survival instinct. Default colony intelligence without a mimicked complex nervous system is approximately equivalent to a colonial insect hive.

When the colony consumes an organism with a sufficiently complex nervous system and successfully replicates it, the collective awakens to a higher order of cognition. This acquired sapience is overlaid on the colony's baseline instincts; survival and feeding drives remain present and surface under sufficient stress, producing the feral state.

The digestive, immune, and reproductive systems are not discrete; all three functions are performed by the same cellular process. Individual cells locate foreign biological material, consume it, and split into additional chimera cells. The colony takes on the morphological characteristics of consumed material through this process over time, producing a hybrid body reflecting the sum of the colony's dietary history.

Xenochimera do not reproduce sexually. Reproductive organs serve no functional purpose for the colony and are typically absent. New colonies form when sufficient chimera biomass is seeded into a fresh body; the cells consume the donor and restructure into a new colony. The process is not well understood.

Body reconstruction is a natural process that occurs during rest. The colony cannibalizes unnecessary structure and grows replacements. Drastic reconstruction or repair from severe damage requires hibernation, during which the body reverts to base cellular state while the outer skin remains in place as a shell. Reconstruction from severe damage takes days to weeks. Rapid reconstruction under pressure is possible in minutes but produces flawed bodies with structural errors that accumulate unless properly rebuilt during a later full reconstruction cycle.

## Origins

The first known encounter with xenochimera biological material was the survey vessel *Roanoake*, which suffered a meteoroid impact in 2382 CE while traversing the Scutum-Centaurus arm. The crew scuttled the ship after contracting an unidentified contagion and ejected the black box rather than risk transmission.

The connection between the *Roanoake* incident and subsequent disease reports at other locations was not established until 2456, when sample comparison across three separate sites revealed identical microorganisms in asteroids spanning thousands of light-years. The microorganism was named Roanoake Syndrome. Laboratory testing confirmed the organisms mimicked other microbes but were too unpredictable for medical application.

In 2514, a macroorganism was identified as an asymptomatic carrier of Roanoake Syndrome, showing hybrid characteristics of several indigenous life forms. Every cell in its body was a Roanoake microbe mimicking its cellular counterpart. This organism was designated Xeno-Chimera 1. Within three years, the first laboratory-created macroorganism combining white mouse and cockroach structure was produced.

In 2542, the first confirmed sapient xenochimera was identified. Others were subsequently found, including individuals who had been living among frontier settlements for years, recognized as unusual life forms but not identified as xenochimera.

The origin point of xenochimera biomass is unknown. The most widely accepted theory involves a catastrophic impact that shattered a home planet and scattered biosphere material into interstellar space. The high Alden-Sarapova particle count found in a 2542 Virgo-Erigone asteroid of geologically young age suggests some dispersal events involve bluespace exposure rather than only conventional ballistic dispersal.

## Feral State

Feral episodes occur when stress factors accumulate beyond a threshold. Primary triggers include pain, hunger, and intense fear or overstimulation. These factors compound; a hungry xenochimera is significantly more likely to enter feral state from additional stress than a well-fed and uninjured one. Less stimulus is required to maintain feral state than to induce it.

A feral xenochimera regresses to animal cognition. Behavior is that of a frightened, possibly hungry, potentially injured wild animal in an unfamiliar environment. The individual will prefer to hide in low-light areas. They will not operate advanced equipment. Stun weapons cause pain and worsen feral state rather than calming it. Feeding addresses the hunger trigger; removing injury stimuli addresses the pain trigger; reducing crowd pressure and loud stimuli addresses fear triggers. Resolving all triggers is required before the individual begins to recover.

The most effective calming measures are feeding meat (the closer to fresh, the more effective), removing sources of stimulation, and providing a dark, quiet space. A nervous system depressant combined with meat, colloquially called a Monster Tamer in service settings, has demonstrated accelerated calming compared to feeding alone.

## Life Cycle

Young chimera colonies grow from whatever biomass is available: cast-off material from an existing colony, infected host tissue, or seeded contact with life-bearing environments. An initial growth phase involves rapid adaptation and experimentation as new material is incorporated. Most chimera eventually stabilize around a body configuration that works for them.

Body modification occurs naturally during rest. Major changes require hibernation in a base cellular state, lasting days to weeks. The chimera is vulnerable during hibernation and typically seeks concealment before beginning the process.

## Interspecies Relations

Xenochimera face widespread suspicion across most of known space. The fact that a sapient xenochimera necessarily consumed a complex nervous system at some point in its development is widely known and contributes to social difficulty. Those raised in environments with access to synthetic or animal-derived nervous tissue, or in cultures with established frameworks for xenochimera integration, tend to have less difficulty.

NanoTrasen permits xenochimera crew on stations where crew acceptance is sufficient. A Protean Access Network permit is not required for xenochimera, distinguishing them from protean synthetics despite superficial behavioral similarities.

## Medical and Operational Notes

**Standard treatment:** Xenochimera mimicking standard physiology are treated with conventional protocols for most injury types. Wounds, toxins, and organ damage respond to standard medical intervention.

**Physical damage profile:** Xenochimera are more resilient to blunt and physical trauma than most species. Burn and thermal damage is disproportionately effective against them; fire-based injuries will present as more severe than expected. Injury severity assessments should account for this asymmetry.

**Infection immunity:** Xenochimera cannot be infected by pathogens.

**Cold temperature:** Low temperatures produce torpor rather than damage. Cryocell treatment is effective for stabilization but produces significant cognitive impairment on recovery; the individual may need extended time to regain normal function.

**Cloning and body printing:** Xenochimera cannot be cloned by any currently available technology. Body printing is not compatible with their biology.

**Revival from apparent death:** A xenochimera that appears dead may still contain viable cells. Inject 15 units of animal protein to prompt cellular recovery. Oral administration requires double the quantity. If protein is unavailable, 30 units of standard nutriment injected (or 60 administered orally) provides partial benefit. Recovery accelerates with addition of 5 units of Clonexadone and an equal quantity of Monster Tamer in the bloodstream simultaneously. The reviving individual will likely be in feral state and will need a secure recovery space.

**Spaceacillin:** Effective at putting Roanoake Syndrome infections into remission if non-chimera crew are exposed to primitive chimera cell material.

**Stun weapons:** Energy-based stun weapons are significantly more painful to xenochimera than to most species and will worsen feral state. Net-type capture weapons are substantially less harmful. Sedative injection is the least harmful restraint option.
