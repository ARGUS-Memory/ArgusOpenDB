# FTL Travel

Faster-than-light travel underpins human civilization across known space. All current FTL methods exploit the properties of bluespace in some form, and all require phoron as fuel. The distribution of stable subspace particle streams has shaped interstellar trade routes much as river systems shaped early terrestrial civilizations.

---

## Bluespace Drives

### Subspace Flux Drive

The oldest and most widely used FTL technology. Subspace flux drives encapsulate their subject in a protective field and exploit naturally occurring subspace particles to achieve rapid transit. Each jump requires a predetermined amount of energy, phoron, and charge time, during which the drive waits to encounter subspace particles of sufficient energy and vector.

Energy consumption and charge time scale exponentially with distance, making short jumps more economical than long ones. An in-system jump requires substantial electrical energy but is achievable with solid-phoron engines. Jumps between stars require a large supermatter or fusion reactor to power the drive.

Subspace flux drives function only where natural subspace particle density is adequate. In regions of low density, known as dead space, a ship using only a flux drive risks becoming stranded. Most settled space has been shaped around access to stable subspace particle streams for exactly this reason.

### Phoron Compression Drive

Phoron compression drives generate their own subspace particles through significantly higher phoron consumption, requiring only a major gravity well to operate. This allows transit through regions of low natural subspace density without risk of stranding, as long as fuel holds. The trade-off is longer charge times and practical limitation to neighboring system jumps before fuel costs become infeasible.

Commercial phoron compression drives have been available for roughly a century. Most ships carry one as a backup drive for navigational emergencies or travel within systems with irregular subspace density. Exploration-class vessels, particularly those operating through dead space, rely on compression drives as their primary propulsion.

### Bluespace Warpgates

Bluespace warpgates are massive fixed structures enabling transit between linked gates at extreme speed and fuel efficiency. They function by creating stable openings to bluespace, with most FTL transit occurring just inside bluespace to avoid realspace limitations. A properly maintained warpgate reduces weeks of transit to days.

Warpgate construction requires proximity to a massive singularity in a region of high subspace particle density. Construction takes decades and costs are extraordinary. Warpgates must also be built with their bluespace components in near-perfect alignment with existing warpgates, or risk stranding travelers within bluespace.

Three public warpgates exist within SolGov space. A fourth near the Vir system has been proposed but not yet constructed, which significantly affects NanoTrasen's operational dominance in that system.

## Practical Considerations

### Ship Scale and Transit Time

Bluespace transit is, counterintuitively, faster for larger ships. While larger vessels require more energy per jump, they can mount proportionally larger generators and capacitors, allowing more efficient routes. For interstellar travel, large ships are effectively the only economical option. Small ships occasionally make interstellar crossings, but point-to-point gateways remain too expensive for most such applications.

Within a system, smaller shuttles dominate. They are cheaper to produce, more maneuverable around gravitational variations, and well-suited to the frequent short hops that in-system travel demands. Most inhabited systems maintain at least one major spaceport handling transfers between in-system shuttles and interstellar vessels.

### Logistics and Routing

Geometrically, the shortest route between two points is a straight line, but subspace particle gradients make direct routes impractical for longer journeys. The fastest routes typically detour to follow stable subspace particle streams, which are faster and safer even when they add distance.

Waypoint systems along major transit corridors serve both logistical and economic functions: they allow crew and passengers rest, enable cargo reshuffling, and allow smaller vessels to make long voyages by breaking them into shorter legs. The economic value of waypoint positions has created and sustained many colonies established purely for their geographic convenience.

### Dangers

Modern navigation is statistically safe, but FTL travel is not without hazard. Miscalculated jumps that bring a ship within a solid body result in catastrophic overlap damage. Miscalibrated shielding can irradiate a vessel or strip the outer layers of its hull. These errors are rare with standard drives but become more common with experimental or bleeding-edge technology.

Navigational error is more common than mechanical failure. Subspace density can change substantially with relatively minor positional changes, and mapping every point between two stars is not feasible. Dead space regions, where no safe paths have been mapped, present the risk of becoming stranded. Dead space is known to be prevalent near the Rarkajar Rift and in portions of Unathi Hegemony territory.

### Regulations

Spacecraft registration in SolGov space is handled by the Office of Transit. All space-capable vessels must carry a prefix designation, a registered name, and a unique identification number. See [Ship Naming](ShipNaming.md) for prefix conventions and registration procedures.

SolGov's approach to licensing has alternated between restrictive and permissive periods. The current era is more restrictive, following security concerns from the Unathi Wars period. Most independent operators are second- or third-generation holders of their vessel registration. Smaller shuttlecraft are substantially easier to license, which has contributed to the rise of carrier mega-liners that transport personal shuttles between systems.
