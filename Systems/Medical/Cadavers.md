# Cadavers

This article covers the handling, processing, and forensic analysis of deceased crew members, including body transport protocols, autopsy procedure, cause-of-death assessment, and post-autopsy documentation.

---

## Body Handling

When a deceased crew member is discovered, security and medical are notified before the body is moved. Medical personnel do not relocate the body without clearance from security, as the scene may be relevant to an ongoing investigation.

Transport uses a body bag or a roller bed rather than direct handling. Body bags can be labelled with a pen. An organised morgue with labelled bags reduces questions and simplifies case management. If the morgue becomes full after autopsies and resleeving are complete, the chaplain can be requested to arrange cremation of unclaimed remains.

---

## Resurrection Options

Two methods exist for returning a deceased crew member to service:

**Resleeving** is the standard procedure. A medical doctor grows and prepares a new body using the resleeving equipment. The Research Director can assist if no doctor is available. After resleeving the crew member will experience symptoms consistent with [Non-Contiguous Memory Disorder](NCMD.md), particularly regarding the circumstances of their death.

**Cyborgification** is a last resort when resleeving is not viable. A roboticist extracts the brain and places it in an MMI for transfer to a cyborg chassis. A surgeon can perform the extraction if no roboticist is present. The Research Director supervises the procedure if neither specialist is available.

## Autopsy Procedure

The morgue operating table is used for autopsy work. Surgeons and forensic technicians perform autopsies.

1. Place the cadaver on the operating table.
2. Scan the cadaver with a health analyser to identify injured body regions.
3. In the damage zone selector, aim for an affected area.
4. Apply a scalpel to open the skin over that area. The chest region may require two incisions to fully expose.
5. Apply the autopsy scanner to the opened area to record findings.
6. Repeat steps 3 through 5 for all other affected areas.

Right-clicking the autopsy scanner after completing all scans provides the option to print a preliminary autopsy report.

---

## Autopsy Report

The printed report contains mechanically recorded findings but is incomplete without supplemental notes. The following fields are recorded manually with a pen:

- Final vital statistics at time of death: brute damage, burn damage, toxin damage, suffocation damage
- Cause of death if not apparent from the scan data
- Name of the crew member who transported the body to medical
- Whether the cadaver was resleeved or cyborgified, and the name of the specialist who performed it
- Name of the crew member who performed the autopsy
- Signature of the preparer

A copy of the completed report is filed in the morgue's autopsy report cabinet. A second copy is delivered to security, preferably directly to the detective.

## Causes of Death

Autopsy data does not always identify cause of death directly. The following patterns are used in conjunction with scan results, visual examination, and scene information to determine cause.

### Decapitation

The head was removed before other injuries reached lethal thresholds.

- Total recorded damage across all regions does not reach lethal levels.
- No head is present on the cadaver.

### De-Braining

The brain was removed while the crew member was alive.

- Visual examination shows no brain present.
- The skull shows evidence of prior surgical entry.

### Burst Appendix

The appendix ruptured internally, causing sepsis and eventual suffocation.

- Toxin damage is present, but no toxic compounds appear in the bloodstream.
- Minor brute damage is recorded to the groin region.
- Suffocation damage is severe.
- The body was found in contact with emesis.

### Bleeding Out

The crew member lost blood volume to the point of cardiovascular failure.

- The cadaver appears pale.
- A health analyser scan shows critically low blood volume.
- Suffocation damage is present as a secondary effect.
- A body scanner placed over the cadaver reveals locations of internal bleeders if external wounds are insufficient to account for blood loss.

### Malpractice

Death or contributing injury resulting from medical intervention. Several subtypes exist.

**Surgical mishap:** Tool errors during an operation are recorded in the autopsy report. Performing surgery in a non-sterile environment produces infections at the surgical site.

- Visible inflammation at the surgical site.
- Infection is detectable on a health analyser scan.
- The crew member had received surgery prior to death.

**Blood type rejection:** An incompatible blood type was administered during transfusion.

- Toxin damage with no identifiable toxic source.
- Suffocation damage with no identifiable cause.
- The crew member had received a blood transfusion.

**Improper transport:** Movement or handling of the crew member during treatment caused additional injury.

- No abnormal findings on scans.
- Blood trails at locations where the cadaver was previously positioned indicate the injury occurred during transport.

**Medication overdose:** An excessive dose of a pharmacological compound was administered.

- Severe toxin damage recorded.
- No harmful chemical compounds appear in a standard toxin scan.
- An advanced spectrometric analysis of the bloodstream reveals elevated levels of the relevant compound.
