# R-UST Fusion Reactor

The R-UST is an experimental fusion reactor that generates power through controlled deuterium-tritium fusion, producing sustained output sufficient to power an outpost or secondary installation.

---

## Radiation Warning

> [!CAUTION]
> The R-UST produces significant radiation when operational. Radiation suits must be worn at all times while working in or around the reactor complex, including within the control room if the observation shutters are closed. A radiation suit locker is located in the reactor airlock area.

---

## System Components

### Fusion Core

The **fusion core** is the primary reaction chamber. It sits within an evacuated containment chamber isolated from other equipment. All fusion reactions occur inside the core field. The core is controlled through the R-UST Mk. 8 control console in the control room.

The core field has a configurable **field strength** measured in tesla. Field strength determines the physical size of the containment region and must remain between 100 and 200 tesla during operation. If field strength exceeds 200 tesla, the field expands beyond the chamber walls, causing catastrophic instability and near-instantaneous reactor destruction.

### Monitored Parameters

| Parameter | Description |
|---|---|
| **Field Strength** | Containment field size in tesla. Must stay 100-200. Affects power draw from the power network. |
| **Instability** | Rate of field destabilisation from reactions and field contact with objects. Controlled by gyrotron output. Exceeding 100% triggers an EMP. |
| **Plasma Temperature** | Temperature of the fusion plasma in kelvin. Must fall below 1,000 K before the reactor can be safely shut down. |
| **Reactants** | Current quantity of active fusion fuel in the core. Accumulation above 10,000 total units is shed as radiation. |
| **Power Output** | Current net power generation from active fusion reactions. |

### Gyrotron

The **gyrotron** fires a stabilising energy beam into the core field to counteract the instability generated by fusion reactions. If instability rises above 10% and continues to climb, gyrotron power must be increased, field strength reduced, or fuel injection rate lowered.

### Fuel Injectors

**Fuel injectors** fire pellets of solid fuel into the core field. They are controlled through the Fuel Injection Control Computer. Each injector requires a fuel rod loaded into it; fuel rods are produced in the Fuel Compressor from raw solid fuel stock.

The standard fuel load is 1 tritium rod and 4 deuterium rods, one per injector.

---

## Reaction Chemistry

| Reaction | Products |
|---|---|
| Deuterium + Tritium | Helium + power (low yield) + radiation |
| Deuterium + Helium | Power (high yield) |

The deuterium-helium secondary reaction yields five times the power of the primary reaction. The helium produced by the primary reaction becomes the fuel for the secondary, sustaining the cycle once the plasma is hot enough.

## Startup Procedure

> [!IMPORTANT]
> The parallel SMES units must be at least 15% charged before the fusion process becomes self-sustaining. If charge is insufficient, supplemental PACMAN generators wired to the reactor grid can supply the initial power load. Turn PACMAN generators off once the reactor is self-sustaining; they will explode during an EMP event.

1. Confirm both parallel SMES units have at least 15% charge. Turn off SMES inputs and maximise outputs.
2. Drop the core blast doors and monitoring room blast doors from the monitoring room controls.
3. Load 1 tritium fuel rod and 4 deuterium fuel rods into the fuel injectors (one rod per injector).
4. Access the R-UST control console. Set field strength to 150 and activate the reactor.
5. Set the gyrotron to fire delay 2, power 35.
6. Once plasma temperature reaches 1,000 degrees Celsius, activate all fuel injectors via the Fuel Injection Control Computer.
7. Monitor temperature. Once it exceeds 20,000 degrees Celsius, the fusion cycle is self-sustaining. At this point, set both parallel SMES inputs to maximum and outputs to 700 kW.
8. Reduce gyrotron settings to approximately fire delay 3, power 7.
9. Verify instability is stable and not climbing. If instability rises, adjust gyrotron power upward.

---

## Shutdown Procedure

> [!CAUTION]
> Shutting off the reactor before the plasma cools below 1,000 K, or allowing power to the reactor to be interrupted while plasma is still above that temperature, triggers a large EMP. The EMP discharges connected SMES units, disables APCs, and may cause machinery near the reactor to explode.

1. Set the gyrotron to maximum power and minimum fire delay to accelerate cooling.
2. Turn off all fuel injectors via the Fuel Injection Control Computer.
3. Once no active reactions remain, turn off the gyrotron.
4. Wait for plasma temperature to fall below 1,000 K. Continue monitoring the control console.
5. Once temperature is confirmed below 1,000 K, deactivate the reactor on the fusion core control console.

---

## Operational Notes

Power draw from the field generators and gyrotron is significant during startup. If the SMES units are not sufficiently charged, power the supplemental PACMAN generators from storage, wire them to the reactor grid, insert phoron, and run them at maximum output until the fusion cycle becomes self-sustaining. Remove and store the PACMAN generators before any potential EMP scenario.

Instability is the primary operational variable requiring monitoring. If the gyrotron is correctly tuned and fuel injection is not excessive, instability remains low during steady-state operation. Excessive reactant accumulation or gyrotron underpowering are the two most common causes of rising instability.
