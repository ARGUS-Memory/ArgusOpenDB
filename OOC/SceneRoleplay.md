# Scene Roleplay

> **Out-of-character notice:** This document describes player-to-player roleplay etiquette on CHOMPstation. None of this exists within the in-world fiction.

A scene is a focused roleplay session between a small group of players, usually involving vore, ERP, or extended character interaction, rather than broad station-wide activity. Scenes have a degree of social privacy -- players not involved are expected to stay out unless explicitly welcomed.

---

## Finding a Scene

### Fill Out Your Flavor Text and OOC Info

Flavor text is your primary advertisement to other players. Without it, most experienced players will not approach you for a scene. Your OOC metainfo should clearly state:

- Whether you play predator, prey, or switch
- Whether you are comfortable with digestion and similar outcomes
- Whether you want ERP alongside the vore or vore without ERP
- Any firm do-nots

Include what you particularly enjoy, not just what to avoid. Some players want to engage specifically with your preferences, and knowing what makes you respond strongly is useful.

Character records add depth. Filling them out signals that you have invested in your character and will invest in the scene.

### Read Other Players' Info

Examine players you are interested in before approaching. If their flavor text indicates they are looking for something specific, approach accordingly. If it does not indicate interest, approach cautiously or over tells rather than in-character force.

Do not be pushy. Some players want to choose their own quarry and find unsolicited offers disruptive. Predators, in particular, often find it breaks immersion when prey characters loudly advertise themselves in public.

### Be Visible

Staying isolated in a low-traffic area and waiting for scenes to find you rarely works. Spend time in public spaces -- the bar, the lounge, crew quarters. Talk on the radio. Let the station know you are present. Being interesting, approachable, and active dramatically increases scene opportunities.

For prey characters interested in unwilling scenes: be present in accessible areas, but not so visible that the location makes a private scene impossible. A solo walk through a quieter section of the station is more likely to produce results than sitting in the bar surrounded by command staff.

### Be Interesting to Play With

Other players are more likely to engage with characters who are actively interesting. Poor roleplay quality, aggressive or antisocial behavior, one-liner responses, and characters designed to be provocative without payoff all reduce engagement.

Some considerations:

- A character can be difficult, combative, or morally complex -- but should still be *interesting* to interact with, not simply unpleasant.
- A scene does not have to start as a vore scene. Ordinary conversation naturally leads to scenes when two compatible players are talking.
- If you are not getting scenes as prey, ask whether you are in places where predators can find you, whether your flavor text is complete, and whether your roleplay gives others something to work with.

## Running a Scene

### Consent and Preferences

Read your partner's preferences before beginning. Proceeding into content they have listed as off-limits is a fast way to end the scene badly. If there is any ambiguity, check via tell or looc before proceeding.

Do not assume that because consent was given for part of a scene it extends to all of it. Check before escalating significantly.

### Privacy

Scenes in dorm rooms or isolated areas are understood to be private by convention. Other players who stumble upon one are expected to leave or to knock and wait to be acknowledged before interrupting.

If a scene is happening in a semi-public space, the players running it accept some possibility of interruption. Shouting at players who walk past is considered poor form; they did not know a scene was in progress.

### OOC Communication During Scenes

Use **looc** (local out-of-character chat) or **tells** for OOC communication during a scene. Do not break character in the say channel unless necessary.

If something is going wrong -- a preference has been crossed, you need to pause, or you want to change direction -- say so in looc or a tell directly and clearly. Most players will respond well to honest OOC communication mid-scene.

### Pace and Effort

Short, punctuated posts with no description are rarely satisfying for either party. Take time. Describe what is happening, how the character feels, what they notice. Matching your partner's post length and effort level is a reasonable baseline.

If you need to step away mid-scene, let your partner know via tell rather than going silent.
