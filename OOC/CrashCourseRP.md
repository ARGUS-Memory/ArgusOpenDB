# A Crash Course in Roleplaying

> **Out-of-character notice:** This document covers roleplay technique and server expectations. None of this exists within the in-world fiction.

CHOMPstation enforces medium-to-heavy roleplay. This guide covers the core discipline, common failure modes, and practical technique for new and returning players.

---

## The Core Rule: Your Character Is Not You

You know only what your character knows. If you are playing an Intern, your character does not know how to start the engine, perform surgery, or identify syndicate equipment -- even if you personally know how all of those things work. Conversely, if your character is a trained Medical Doctor, they can perform complex procedures even if you personally do not know how to execute them mechanically.

When your character faces something outside their knowledge, roleplay the uncertainty. Try the action badly. Ask for help in-character. Being visibly competent at things your character has no background in is a metagaming tell.

---

## Metagaming

Metagaming is using out-of-character knowledge to influence in-character decisions.

**Common violations:**

- Using information from the OOC channel in-character
- Identifying a person as a traitor, cultist, or agent based on OOC knowledge of the game mode
- Calling suspicious items by their mechanical names when your character would have no basis for that identification (a mysterious package is not "C4" to your character)
- Using information from a previous life or a previous round that your character would not remember

Do not say "traitor," "cultist," "revs," or "revolutionaries" unless your character has been directly told this information by another character in-world. Seeing someone commit a crime gives your character knowledge that a crime occurred, not knowledge of the perpetrator's faction.

## Getting In Character

Think about your character as a specific person with consistent traits:

- Are they shy or outgoing with strangers?
- How obedient are they toward superiors?
- How do they react to violence, injury, or death?
- What are they afraid of? What do they want?

**Characters that never show fear, pain, stress, or hesitation are not well-played -- they are players playing themselves with a different name.** Try reactions. Wince at pain. Show nerves. Express opinions about things. Talk about the food, the station layout, the strange new crew member. Idle conversation makes characters feel real and opens natural paths to scenes.

A few techniques to practice:
- React physically to events (use `me` emotes for wincing, stepping back, looking uncomfortable)
- Ask questions that fit your character's knowledge level
- Bring up off-topic IC subjects during work tasks to let personality emerge

---

## Staying In Character Around Stress

**Do not break character in public channels when things go badly.** If you are arrested, do not complain on OOC. If you are being processed by security, cooperate or resist in-character but keep the dispute inside the fiction. If you believe a rule was broken, use **Adminhelp (F1)** after the situation, not the OOC channel during it.

If a situation calls for your character to do something they are afraid of or inexperienced at, lean into that rather than ignoring it. A character who runs toward every hazard as confidently as a trained commando is unconvincing.

---

## Communicating With Others

Roleplay is a cooperative activity. Useful habits:

- **Always communicate.** Tell people what you are doing, what you want, what your character is thinking about. Real people narrate and check in with those around them.
- **Defer to your superiors** in-character unless you have an in-world reason not to. Department heads, security officers, and command staff have authority your character is expected to respect.
- **Do not run from security** unless your character has committed a crime and has an in-world motivation to flee. Innocent people do not bolt when asked a question.
- **Match post length.** If your partner is writing paragraph-length posts, one-liners are not a proportionate response.

---

## Antagonists

**Most antagonist factions are unknown to the general crew.** The average crew member has never heard of the Syndicate and would not recognize their equipment. If you encounter suspicious behavior, your character notices a suspicious person, not a traitor operative.

When running antagonist roles:

- Roleplay with your targets. A silent ambush and drag is technically effective but rarely satisfying for the other party.
- The goal is not to win -- it is to create an engaging experience for everyone involved, including the people opposing you.
- If you need to act quickly, adminhelp before or after to give context to admins.

---

## Command Roles

Heads of staff oversee their department rather than performing every task themselves. As a head of staff:

- Delegate routine work to your team.
- Issue orders with rationale rather than unexplained directives.
- Take responsibility for your department's performance.
- Hold regular check-ins, delegate to active crew, and document decisions where appropriate.
- Bear consequences for your department's failures as well as their successes.

As Colony Director: you carry final responsibility for the station. Your leadership style -- rigid and procedural, or informal and approachable -- shapes the entire station's atmosphere.

---

## Security and Suspicion

Security officers investigate suspicious behavior. Cooperating with a security question or pat-down is not an admission of guilt. Refusing routine compliance, fleeing at the sight of security, or carrying obviously out-of-place items are all behaviors that give security legitimate grounds for escalation.

If you have done nothing wrong and are stopped, say so calmly and explain. Shouting, running, or arguing that the stop is unfair reads as suspicious behavior regardless of whether it is.
